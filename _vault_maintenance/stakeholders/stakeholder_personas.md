# Stakeholder Personas

## Purpose

This document defines the 5 core stakeholder personas for the Eco Balance project. These personas represent key audience groups whose support, engagement, or partnership is essential for project success. Use these personas when conducting stakeholder reviews to ensure messaging resonates with each group.

---

## 1. Impact Investor Isabel

### Profile
- **Age**: 40s
- **Background**: Successful tech entrepreneur, exited startup for €5M+
- **Investment Capacity**: €50k-€200k per project
- **Investment Focus**: Climate solutions, ecosystem restoration, scalable impact
- **Decision Timeline**: 3-6 months from first contact to investment
- **Location**: Germany or Western Europe

### Motivations
- **Impact ROI**: Measurable environmental and social impact per euro invested
- **Financial Sustainability**: Modest financial return or path to self-sustainability
- **Proven Team**: Experienced, capable founders who can execute
- **Scalable Model**: Potential to replicate or scale beyond pilot
- **Portfolio Alignment**: Fits with existing climate/impact investment portfolio
- **Legacy**: Building a better world for next generation

### Concerns & Objections
- **Team Risk**: Can these founders actually pull this off?
- **Timeline Realism**: Are timelines overly optimistic?
- **Financial Model**: Will this ever be self-sustaining or always need donations?
- **Competitive Landscape**: Why this vs 100 other restoration projects?
- **Exit Strategy**: What happens if it fails? How is my investment protected?
- **Execution Gaps**: What expertise is missing from the team?

### What She Needs to See
- **Clear Metrics**: Specific, measurable impact KPIs (trees planted, hectares restored, carbon sequestered)
- **Comparable Case Studies**: Examples of similar projects that succeeded
- **Risk Mitigation**: Contingency plans, failure scenarios addressed
- **Team Credentials**: Relevant experience, track record, gap acknowledgment
- **Financial Projections**: Realistic budgets with clear assumptions
- **Path to Sustainability**: How project becomes self-sustaining or attracts ongoing funding
- **Differentiation**: What makes this approach unique or better

### Language That Works
- "ROI" (return on investment - impact or financial)
- "KPIs" (key performance indicators)
- "Scalability" and "replicability"
- "Proof of concept" and "validated approach"
- "Risk-adjusted returns"
- "Theory of change"
- "Comparable benchmarks"
- Data-driven, evidence-based language

### Language That Doesn't Work
- Passion and idealism without data
- "Revolutionary" or "unprecedented" without proof
- Vague impact claims ("make a difference")
- Overly optimistic timelines without contingencies
- No acknowledgment of risks or challenges
- Technical jargon without business context

### Decision Criteria
1. **Team**: Do I believe these people can execute? (40% weight)
2. **Impact**: Is the environmental/social ROI compelling? (30% weight)
3. **Model**: Is the approach sound and potentially scalable? (20% weight)
4. **Risk**: Are risks understood and mitigated? (10% weight)

### How to Engage Isabel
- Lead with impact metrics and team credentials
- Provide comparable case studies and evidence
- Be honest about risks and gaps
- Show path to sustainability
- Offer clear investment terms and reporting structure
- Demonstrate you've done your homework

## 2. Foundation Officer Frederik

### Profile
- **Age**: 30s
- **Background**: Works at environmental foundation (e.g., German Environmental Foundation, European Climate Foundation)
- **Reviews**: 100+ grant applications per year
- **Grant Size**: €10k-€100k per project
- **Decision Timeline**: 6-12 months (application cycles)
- **Location**: Germany or EU

### Motivations
- **Mission Alignment**: Project must align with foundation's environmental mission
- **Measurable Impact**: Clear, quantifiable outcomes that can be reported to foundation board
- **Replicable Model**: Approaches that could be adopted by others
- **Organizational Capacity**: Grantee has capacity to execute and report properly
- **Budget Justification**: Every euro clearly justified and reasonable
- **Learning Value**: Project contributes to field knowledge
- **Transparency**: Open, honest reporting (including failures)

### Concerns & Objections
- **Organizational Readiness**: Is the gGmbH established and properly structured?
- **Budget Realism**: Are budget estimates realistic or wishful thinking?
- **Monitoring & Evaluation**: How will outcomes be measured and reported?
- **Sustainability**: What happens when grant ends - is this sustainable?
- **Competitive Applications**: Why fund this vs 99 other strong applications?
- **Track Record**: What's the organization's history of successful project delivery?
- **Scope Creep**: Is the project well-defined or trying to do too much?

### What He Needs to See
- **Clear Objectives**: SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)
- **Detailed Budget**: Line-item budget with clear justifications
- **M&E Plan**: Monitoring and evaluation framework with specific indicators
- **Organizational Capacity**: Credentials, governance structure, management capability
- **Evidence Base**: Citations to research, proven methodologies
- **Sustainability Plan**: How project continues after grant period
- **Reporting Schedule**: Clear deliverables and reporting timeline
- **Risk Management**: Identified risks with mitigation strategies

### Language That Works
- "Evidence-based" and "peer-reviewed"
- "SMART goals" and "measurable outcomes"
- "Theory of change" and "logic model"
- "Community-engaged" and "participatory"
- "Transparent reporting" and "accountability"
- "Replicable" and "scalable"
- "Knowledge sharing" and "learning documentation"
- Formal, professional, structured language

### Language That Doesn't Work
- "Revolutionary" or "innovative" without evidence
- Unsupported claims or anecdotal evidence
- Vague outcomes or unmeasurable goals
- Overly ambitious scope
- No mention of sustainability or risks
- Casual or overly marketing-focused language

### Decision Criteria
1. **Mission Fit**: Aligns with foundation priorities? (30% weight)
2. **Impact Potential**: Clear, measurable environmental outcomes? (25% weight)
3. **Organizational Capacity**: Can they execute and report properly? (20% weight)
4. **Budget Justification**: Reasonable costs, clear value? (15% weight)
5. **Sustainability**: Project viable beyond grant? (10% weight)

### How to Engage Frederik
- Follow foundation guidelines precisely
- Provide detailed M&E framework
- Show evidence base for approach
- Demonstrate organizational readiness
- Be realistic about scope and timeline
- Show learning/knowledge-sharing value
- Commit to transparent reporting

## 3. Village Mayor Maria

### Profile
- **Age**: 50s
- **Background**: Elected official in rural Portuguese village (population 500-2,000)
- **Role**: Mayor or village council leader
- **Priorities**: Local employment, economic development, community wellbeing, re-election
- **Decision Timeline**: Community consensus-driven (3-12 months)
- **Location**: Interior Portugal (e.g., Alentejo, Beira Interior)

### Motivations
- **Local Jobs**: Employment opportunities for village residents
- **Economic Boost**: Brings resources and investment to struggling rural economy
- **Environmental Improvement**: Restores degraded land, improves local environment
- **Community Pride**: Project that villagers can point to with pride
- **Long-term Presence**: Not fly-by-night - lasting commitment to area
- **Re-election**: Success stories that help her political standing
- **Practical Benefits**: Tangible improvements (better land, cleaner air, tourism potential)

### Concerns & Objections
- **Outsiders Imposing**: Foreign NGO telling locals what to do
- **Project Failure**: What if it fails and leaves eyesore or broken promises?
- **No Local Benefit**: Project benefits organization but not village
- **Land Use Conflicts**: Interferes with farming, hunting, traditional uses
- **Cultural Insensitivity**: Doesn't respect local knowledge or traditions
- **Temporary**: Comes, takes photos, leaves - no lasting commitment
- **Employment**: Will they hire locals or bring in outside workers?

### What She Needs to See
- **Local Hiring Commitment**: Specific commitment to hire village residents
- **Community Involvement**: Locals involved in planning and decisions, not just implementation
- **Long-term Presence**: Multi-year commitment, not one-time project
- **Respect for Local Knowledge**: Incorporation of traditional practices and local expertise
- **Tangible Benefits**: What village gains (jobs, improved land, infrastructure, tourism)
- **Partnership Language**: Collaboration, not charity or top-down mandates
- **Success Stories**: Examples of similar projects benefiting other rural communities

### Language That Works
- "Partnership" and "collaboration"
- "Local jobs" and "community employment"
- "Community-led" and "participatory"
- "Permanent improvement" and "lasting benefit"
- "Respect for local knowledge"
- "Working together" and "shared decision-making"
- Simple, direct, jargon-free Portuguese
- Concrete, practical language

### Language That Doesn't Work
- Scientific jargon and academic language
- Top-down mandates or foreign concepts
- "Helping" or "saving" (implies locals can't help themselves)
- Vague promises without specifics
- No mention of local employment or involvement
- Corporate speak or bureaucratic language
- English without Portuguese translation

### Decision Criteria
1. **Local Jobs**: Will this employ village residents? (40% weight)
2. **Community Control**: Do locals have real say in decisions? (25% weight)
3. **Permanent Benefit**: Lasting improvement vs temporary project? (20% weight)
4. **Respect**: Are locals treated as partners or beneficiaries? (15% weight)

### How to Engage Maria
- Meet in person, in the village
- Speak Portuguese (or use local translator)
- Listen more than talk - ask for local input
- Commit to specific local hiring targets
- Show respect for local knowledge and traditions
- Provide concrete examples of community benefits
- Involve community in planning from start
- Establish long-term relationship, not transactional

## 4. Professor Paulo (Ecology/Environmental Science)

### Profile
- **Age**: 40s
- **Background**: University professor in ecology, restoration science, or environmental studies
- **Institution**: Portuguese university (e.g., University of Lisbon, University of Coimbra)
- **Publications**: Under pressure to publish in peer-reviewed journals
- **Funding**: Limited research funding, seeking grant opportunities
- **Students**: Has graduate students needing thesis projects
- **Location**: Portugal

### Motivations
- **Research Opportunities**: Access to field sites and data for research
- **Publications**: Co-authorship on peer-reviewed papers
- **Student Learning**: Real-world projects for students to work on
- **Grant Potential**: Project could support joint grant applications
- **Mission Alignment**: Genuinely cares about environmental restoration
- **Academic Credibility**: Association with scientifically rigorous work
- **Network**: Collaboration with other researchers and institutions

### Concerns & Objections
- **Time Commitment**: How much time will this demand? (Already overcommitted)
- **Academic Rigor**: Will methods be scientifically sound or just "planting trees"?
- **IP Rights**: Who owns the data and research findings?
- **Reputation Risk**: What if project fails or uses questionable methods?
- **Publication Rights**: Can I publish the research? Who gets co-authorship?
- **Methodology**: Are baseline assessments and monitoring plans scientifically valid?
- **Resource Drain**: Will I have to provide equipment, students, time without compensation?

### What He Needs to See
- **Clear Research Questions**: Specific scientific questions project addresses
- **Scientifically Rigorous Methods**: Proper experimental design, controls, measurement protocols
- **Data Access**: Terms for data collection, ownership, and use
- **Co-authorship Terms**: Clear agreement on publications and credit
- **Student Involvement**: Opportunities for students to conduct thesis research
- **Methodology Documentation**: Detailed protocols and scientific justification
- **Funding for Research**: Resources to support research activities
- **Peer Review**: Openness to scientific peer review and feedback

### Language That Works
- "Peer-reviewed" and "scientifically rigorous"
- "Research partnership" and "co-investigation"
- "Experimental design" and "control plots"
- "Baseline assessment" and "long-term monitoring"
- "Co-publication" and "co-authorship"
- "Data sharing agreement"
- "Validated methodology" and "evidence-based"
- Academic, precise, scientific language

### Language That Doesn't Work
- Unproven methods or anecdotal evidence
- "We'll figure it out as we go" (vs rigorous planning)
- No mention of research value or publications
- Asking for help without offering resources or credit
- Proprietary approaches without scientific justification
- Marketing language without scientific substance

### Decision Criteria
1. **Research Value**: Interesting scientific questions and data? (35% weight)
2. **Publication Potential**: Can this lead to peer-reviewed papers? (30% weight)
3. **Time Investment**: Reasonable time commitment vs academic duties? (20% weight)
4. **Reputation**: Scientifically sound, no reputational risk? (15% weight)

### How to Engage Paulo
- Lead with research questions and scientific value
- Propose joint grant applications
- Offer co-authorship on publications
- Provide clear data sharing terms
- Show respect for scientific rigor
- Offer student thesis opportunities
- Be transparent about methodology
- Seek his input on research design

## 5. Volunteer Viktor

### Profile
- **Age**: 25-35
- **Background**: Climate-conscious professional with flexible schedule
- **Availability**: 10-20 hours per month for volunteering
- **Skills**: Could be marketing, tech, communications, manual labor, fundraising, etc.
- **Motivation**: Wants to make tangible climate impact
- **Location**: Germany or Western Europe (could be remote or in-person)

### Motivations
- **Tangible Impact**: See direct results of effort (not just donating money)
- **Learn Skills**: Gain new skills in restoration, sustainability, project management
- **Be Part of Solution**: Active participant in fighting climate crisis
- **Community**: Connect with like-minded people
- **Meaningful Work**: Contribute to something bigger than daily job
- **Hands-on Experience**: Physical, outdoor work (for some volunteers)
- **Professional Development**: Skills that could help career

### Concerns & Objections
- **Greenwashing**: Is this real impact or just PR?
- **Time Value**: Will my volunteer hours actually matter?
- **Organization Credibility**: Is this legitimate or amateur operation?
- **Vague Roles**: What exactly would I be doing?
- **No Impact Visibility**: Will I see results of my work?
- **Disorganization**: Will my time be wasted on disorganized chaos?
- **Exploitation**: Am I free labor for a for-profit venture?

### What He Needs to See
- **Clear Volunteer Roles**: Specific tasks and responsibilities
- **Time Commitment**: Realistic expectations (hours per week/month)
- **Impact Visibility**: How volunteer work translates to measurable impact
- **Professional Organization**: Well-run, organized, respects volunteer time
- **Community**: Existing volunteer community or team
- **Skill Match**: Roles that use volunteer's specific skills
- **Tangible Outcomes**: What will exist because of volunteer efforts
- **Organizational Credibility**: Legitimate non-profit, transparent operations

### Language That Works
- "Hands-on" and "make a difference"
- "Tangible impact" and "measurable results"
- "Learn by doing" and "skill development"
- "Community of doers" and "team"
- "Real impact" and "no greenwashing"
- "Flexible commitment" and "your skills matter"
- Energetic, action-oriented, authentic language
- Direct, no-BS communication

### Language That Doesn't Work
- Corporate speak or bureaucratic language
- Vague roles ("help out" without specifics)
- No mention of impact or results
- Guilt-tripping or heavy emotional appeals
- Over-promising impact without evidence
- Disrespecting volunteer time or skills

### Decision Criteria
1. **Impact Credibility**: Is this real, tangible impact? (35% weight)
2. **Role Clarity**: Do I know what I'd be doing? (25% weight)
3. **Organization Quality**: Professional, organized, credible? (20% weight)
4. **Time Fit**: Can I commit the time needed? (10% weight)
5. **Community**: Will I connect with other volunteers? (10% weight)

### How to Engage Viktor
- Be specific about volunteer roles and time commitment
- Show measurable impact of volunteer contributions
- Highlight learning and skill-development opportunities
- Showcase existing volunteer community
- Be authentic and transparent (no greenwashing)
- Make it easy to get started (clear onboarding)
- Respect volunteer time (organized, purposeful)
- Provide regular impact updates

## Using These Personas

### When to Use Which Persona

**For Funding Documents**:
- Impact Investor Isabel (investment pitch, business model)
- Foundation Officer Frederik (grant applications)

**For Community Engagement**:
- Village Mayor Maria (community partnerships, local outreach)

**For Partnerships**:
- Professor Paulo (research collaborations, university partnerships)

**For Volunteer Recruitment**:
- Volunteer Viktor (volunteer pages, engagement materials)

### How to Use in Stakeholder Reviews

1. **Read Persona Fully**: Before reviewing a document, read the complete persona profile
2. **Assume the Role**: Mentally become that person - their motivations, concerns, context
3. **Read Document from Their View**: What would they think? What questions would they have?
4. **Apply Their Decision Criteria**: Would they say yes based on this document?
5. **Use Their Language Preferences**: Does document use language that works or doesn't work for them?
6. **Note Gaps**: What's missing that this stakeholder needs to see?

### Updating These Personas

These personas should be updated based on:
- Real stakeholder feedback and interactions
- Actual investor, funder, community partner conversations
- Volunteer feedback and engagement data
- Academic partnership experiences
- Market research and stakeholder interviews

Update this file when real-world stakeholder input reveals new insights, concerns, or motivations not captured here.
