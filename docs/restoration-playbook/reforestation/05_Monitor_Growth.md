## Purpose

Regular monitoring is essential to track seedling survival, health, and growth. Early detection of problems allows for quick intervention, and data collection provides insights for adaptive management and future projects.

**This is a template.** Customize monitoring schedules, metrics, and methods based on your specific species, site conditions, and project goals.

---

## ðŸŽ¯ Non-Negotiables (Science Consensus)

These must be followed - they are based on scientific consensus:

1. **Monitoring Required:** Tree survival and growth must be monitored. You cannot know if restoration is working without monitoring.

2. **Regular Schedule:** Monitoring must occur on a regular schedule. Irregular monitoring misses problems and trends.

3. **Documentation:** All monitoring data must be documented. This is essential for tracking progress and adaptive management.

4. **Early Detection:** Problems must be detected early. Early intervention is more effective than late intervention.

5. **Adaptive Management:** Monitoring data must be used to inform management decisions. Monitoring without action is ineffective.

## ðŸ“‹ Implementation Steps

### Step 1: Establish Monitoring Schedule

**Frequency:**

**Year 1 (Critical Establishment Period):**
- **Week 1-4:** Every 3-5 days (high mortality risk)
- **Months 2-6:** Weekly
- **Months 7-12:** Bi-weekly to monthly

**Year 2:**
- Monthly during growing season
- Quarterly during dormant season

**Year 3+:**
- Quarterly monitoring
- Annual comprehensive assessment

**Seasonal Considerations:**
- More frequent in summer (heat stress)
- Less frequent in winter (dormancy)
- After extreme weather events (drought, storms, heat waves)

### Step 2: What to Monitor

**Individual Seedling Health:**

**Visual Assessment:**
- âœ… **Healthy:** Green foliage, upright, growing
- âš ï¸ **Stressed:** Wilted, yellowing, stunted
- âŒ **Dead:** Brown/crispy foliage, brittle stems

**Specific Indicators:**

**Positive Signs:**
- New growth (shoots, leaves)
- Healthy leaf color for species
- Firm, resilient stems
- No insect or disease damage
- Growing toward light

**Warning Signs:**
- Wilting despite adequate water
- Discolored leaves (yellowing, browning, spotting)
- Stunted growth
- Insect infestation
- Fungal growth
- Browsing damage (wildlife)

**Critical Problems:**
- Dead or dying seedlings
- Severe defoliation
- Broken or damaged stems
- Root exposure
- Disease spreading through planting

### Step 3: Measure Growth Metrics

**Track These Measurements:**

**Survival Rate:**
- Count living vs. dead seedlings
- Calculate percentage by species
- Identify patterns (location, species, timing)
- Target: >70% Year 1, >60% Year 3

**Height Growth:**
- Measure from ground to tallest point
- Record every 3-6 months
- Track annual growth rate
- Compare to species expectations

**Stem Diameter:**
- Measure at 10 cm above ground (Year 1-2)
- Measure at breast height (1.3m) for older trees
- Track diameter growth
- Indicator of health and vigor

**Crown Development:**
- Assess canopy spread
- Note branching patterns
- Document form and structure
- Compare to species expectations

### Step 4: Document and Analyze

**Record Keeping:**
- Maintain monitoring logs
- Photograph changes over time
- Map problem areas
- Track interventions and results

**Data Analysis:**
- Calculate survival rates
- Track growth trends
- Identify problem patterns
- Compare to expectations

**Adaptive Management:**
- Use data to inform decisions
- Adjust management based on results
- Address problems early
- Learn and improve
